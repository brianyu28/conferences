Seminars

Techniques for Reporters

This seminar will teach students the basics of reporting—including what questions to ask in interviews, how to develop good source relationships, and how to write a daily news article. We’ll given an overview of the different types of reporting as well, including breaking news reporting, beat reporting, and investigative reporting.

Techniques for Editors

Members of The Crimson’s current guard of editors will lead a seminar on how to convert one’s reporting and writing skills into a successful experience as an editor. Along with the basics of how to edit and proofread an article, this seminar will teach students how to be effective leaders at their school newspaper.

Forming the Feature: A Look at Long-Form Journalism

Some news can be covered in five hundred words or less, while other topics warrant extensive in-depth investigation and a more fleshed-out story. This seminar will cover all aspects of writing a news feature—from how to find a good topic, to the research and reporting process, and finally, to structuring and penning the final product.

Column Writing: Making Your Point

Knowing how to form an argument well is an incredibly important and versatile skill, and in this seminar, students will learn how to craft an opinion on a controversial issue. At the same time, Crimson column editors will also teach the basics of column-writing, including how to find a topic and how to establish a unique tone and perspective for a column.

Lookin’ Good: Fundamentals of Design

Attention-grabbing headlines, clean graphics, and striking photos are just a few of the things that go into the making of a good front page. How to organize all of this content in a logical, coherent, and aesthetically-pleasing manner is the challenge that designers face. Students in this seminar will learn the basic principles underlying the layout of the daily newspaper.

Beyond A1: Features Design

From arts and sports sections, to the weekly Fifteen Minutes magazine, to the annual Year in Review issue, The Crimson’s content offers plenty of opportunities for more creative design ideas. This seminar will challenge students to examine a number of prominent (and even award-winning) feature designs from The Crimson’s archives for what makes a design work—and what doesn’t.

The Making of a Magazine: Alternative Ways To Tell a Story

In this seminar, editors of The Crimson’s weekly magazine, Fifteen Minutes, will discuss ways to present campus issues using non-traditional narrative forms. Students will critique and analyze the effectiveness of various pieces from recent issues of FM, and brainstorm creative ways of framing stories for their own school papers.

Photo: Capturing a Thousand Words

How does one tell a story and bring an article to life with a single photo? This seminar will engage students with the question of what makes a photograph visually striking, while at the same time, challenging them to think about photos as journalistic creations. In the process, students will also be introduced to some of the technical skills and terminology to help them broaden their range of skills.

Video: Telling Stories That Move

A well-made video can tell an engaging story on its own or can serve as a valuable complement to a traditional article. In this seminar, participants will learn the basics of creating a video and discuss how to incorporate more innovative multimedia components into their newspapers’ coverage.

What the Critics Are Saying: How To Write an Arts Review

Students will practice how to formulate arguments about an artistic creation and back up their claims with concrete points by sampling various clips from plays, movies, and albums as a group. Then, students will learn how to synthesize their own opinion into a coherent, well-written review.

Hitting Home with the Readers: Sports Column Writing

Current Crimson President Robert S. Samuels will share insights from the writing of “The Book of Samuels,” a Crimson sports column focusing on Harvard’s football team. This seminar will teach students the fundamentals of sports analysis, as well as the basics of column-writing.

Beyond the Game: The Investigative Sports Feature

There’s a certain thrill to covering an athletic event, but the world of sports journalism extends far beyond individual wins and losses. What goes into the making of a winning team, or the training of a successful athlete? This seminar will draw on recent feature stories on Harvard sports teams—including the Harvard men’s basketball team—as examples.

Digging Through Data: The News Graphic

It can be easy for a reporter or reader to get bogged down in the numbers for a data-heavy story, but a chart or other type of graphic can be a clean way to cut through the noise and isolate a particularly important trend or pattern. Members of our design and news teams will teach students how to construct graphics by showing examples from The Crimson’s own archives.

Newspaper 2.0: Online Strategy

Newspapers’ main sources of readership have shifted online, and drawing more users to their websites is a never-ending quest. First, this seminar will introduce a number of features of The Crimson’s own online strategy, including web page analytics, online advertising, and The Crimson’s recent website redesign. Then, students will be challenged to find ways to apply some of the same ideas to their own newspapers’ websites.

Panel: Journalism After High School

Several of our own editors who hope to pursue a career in journalism after college will talk about getting involved in one’s college newspaper and the opportunities available for graduates seeking a career in print.

Journalism in the 21st Century: How to Harness the Power of Social Media

The work of journalists is being rapidly transformed by presence of social media. What exactly does it mean to be a journalist in the 21st century? This seminar will discuss ways in which newspapers utilize new sites such as Facebook and Twitter as a tool for research and reporting, and a platform from which journalists can reach a broader audience.

The Future of the Newspaper as a Business

There are many who say the print newspaper as we know it dead already, and publications are quickly evolving to the changing times. How can publications adjust to ensure that journalism remains a profitable endeavor? And what kind of impact are these trends having on college, and even high school, newspapers?

InDesigning the Paper

Ever find yourself getting bogged down with technical difficulties in the production process? Want to spend less time trying to put stories into the page and more time on the content itself? Our editors have put together an InDesign tutorial to teach you the tricks of the trade.

DISCUSSION GROUPS

Field Reporting Exercise

Students will have an opportunity to practice their reporting skills by interviewing students, tourists, and locals on campus and in the surrounding area. Students will engage with real-life issues that students and Cambridge citizens face while gaining valuable hands-on training by reporting in the field.

Photography Excursion

In this activity, students will venture out into campus as a group with one of The Crimson’s photographers to capture their own shots of historic Harvard buildings in the square, scenic views on the Charles River, and colorful city life in the Square.

Mock Editorial Meeting

The staff editorial is a key part of The Crimson’s Ed Board, and our Editorial Chairs will lead students through a mock editorial meeting. Students will be presented with a set of common controversial school issues up for discussion, and through the course of the meeting, students will debate both sides of each topic and formulate nuanced arguments for potential staff editorials.

News Crisis Simulation

In this exercise, students will be presented with hypothetical “crises” editors and reporters commonly face over the course of their work. Students will be asked for their responses to each different scenario, and our staff will lead them through proper courses of action to resolve each crisis.

Ethics of Journalism Discussion

In this discussion, students will engage in a number of key questions relating to journalistic ethics, including what constitutes libel, anonymous sourcing, and how to cover sensitive issues such as sexual assault and mental health.

Editor-in-Chief Roundtable

Managing editor Rebecca D. Robbins will lead a roundtable for editor-in-chiefs to discuss the responsibilities, challenges, and opportunities that come with managing a team of writers and running a paper.

Faculty Advisor Roundtable

President Robert S. Samuels will lead a group discussion for faculty advisors to share common issues faced by their respective school newspapers. At the end of the discussion, advisors will have the opportunity to take a tour of The Crimson building and learn more about the day-to-day operations of the newspaper.

Summer

When a story is a Story: Newsworthiness

Students will discuss the principles of what makes an article right for their respective news organizations. What elements should one consider when deciding what stories to pursue? Students will learn about the principles of newsworthiness and immediately apply them, debating whether or not a series of pre-selected hypothetical situations should be covered in a local newspaper.

The Writer’s Toolbox

An accomplished Crimson writer will walk students through the writing process, from brainstorming to proofreading, discussing keys to success along the way. Students will learn how to find sources, how to plan and organize a story, and how to use sentence structure to their advantage. We will show good examples to follow for every step of the process.

Interviewing 101

At the end of the day, stories can often only be as good as the information provided by sources. Make sure to get the most out of your interviewees by utilizing the suggestions of an experienced news reporter. Using a mock interview between reporters to discuss crucial moments in every interview, students will learn the ins and outs of journalism’s most crucial activity.

Writing Ethics

Journalism is one of the most ethically demanding professions. Moral issues pop up quite often in this line of work, and having a firm idea of what is right and wrong is essential. Through fun and engaging debates about a number of hypothetical situations, students will find their personal beliefs and discuss what is appropriate behavior for a professional journalist.

Social Media

More and more, reporting is being conducted online on Facebook, Twitter and elsewhere. Speaker will discuss how to ensure you have a professional online reputation, and how to utilize the internet to find stories and sources.

Activities

College Prep

Students will be taken on a guided tour of Harvard College and then come back to The Crimson, where a Harvard student will answer any and all questions about college admissions, college life and reporting in college.

Tour the Globe

Read by over 250,000 in print every day and many many more online, The Boston Globe is one of America’s biggest and most influential papers. Go behind the scenes on a guided tour of The Globe’s facility. See the newsroom, the presses, and sit in on a planning meeting being conducted by the paper’s most important editors.

Brainstorming Exercise

Learn how members of The Crimson come up with stories idea and come up with your own in a brainstorming session. Then get guidance on how to pursue your leads and turn the idea into an article.

Make a Paper

Over the course of a week, students will brainstorm a story idea, report on it, write, and edit, all under the guidance of Crimson editors. By Friday, they will have a special Crimson edition to call their own.
